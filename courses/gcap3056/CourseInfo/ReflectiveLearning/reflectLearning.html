Reflective Learning Journal — Guide

Purpose

Clarify the requirement for the reflective learning journal so you can plan and submit with confidence.

Weighting and assessment

  • 35% of your final grade.
  • Individually assessed (your own journal, not group).
  • Aligns with course outcomes (CILOs 3 & 4): value principles in public discourse, and applying knowledge by writing as informed citizen-scholars.

What we are looking for

  • Learning as lasting change in behaviours and attitudes, not one-off tasks.
  • “Wow” moments: when something in the course surprised or impressed you.
  • “Aha” moments: when you saw how to use course ideas in your project or practice.
  • Depth of reflection and breadth of contribution to the Moodle discussion board (where drafts are shared).

Format and length

  • Required: at least 3 journal entries (Entries 1–3).
  • Optional: up to 2 more entries (Entries 4–5) — we encourage 5 in total, but the last two are optional.
  • Minimum per entry: about 150 words.
  • No upper word limit; write as much as helps you reflect clearly.

Rubrics (Wow & Aha learning)

We look for evidence of learning and human efforts that add value to a human–AI partnership. Permanent changes in behaviours and mindset are the holy grail.

We look for What it means
Evidence of learningYour reflection shows that course ideas or experiences have affected how you think or act, not just that you completed tasks.
Human value in the partnershipYour own thinking, choices, and revision add something that AI alone would not produce—e.g. judgment, critique, connection to your context.
Wow momentsWhen something in the course surprised or impressed you; we see that it mattered to you.
Aha momentsWhen you saw how to use course ideas in your project or practice; we see application, not just description.
Lasting change (behaviours & mindset)The gold standard: signs that your attitudes or habits have shifted in a way that could persist beyond the course.

AI use and human oversight

  • You are encouraged to use AI to draft journal entries.
  • Drafts must be reviewed and refined by you (human oversight).
  • Your partnership with AI should be one of the central themes in the reflective account (as in the course syllabus).

Journal submission and topics (reference)

Please reply in the place indicated on Moodle to submit your journal entries. Details about how to submit will be provided in due course.

The topics below are tentative. You may write journal entries based on your own Wow and Aha moments from the course.

Topic 1: Accessing Information (tentative)

Reflect on the process of getting information from public sources and the government. What did you find particularly interesting, frustrating, or insightful?

Topic 2: Making Recommendations (tentative)

How do you make recommendations on government work and develop strong arguments that may lead to action? How do you resolve the tension between innovation and feasibility (and stability)?

Topic 3: Team Collaboration (tentative)

Reflect on the process of collaborating with peers and the teacher. How did the team create value with different members contributing in different ways?

You should write at least 150 words for each topic. You are encouraged to use AI to draft the journal essays.

This semester’s approach

We have the general philosophy about learning: learning as the permanent change in behaviours and mindset (attitudes) — so we are looking for such changes.

  • Wow: when you are amazed or impressed by something in the course.
  • Aha: when you figure out a way to apply something from the course to your project.

We do some tasks every week; the tasks may help you find the Wow and Aha moments. In around Week 9 each student will have a collection of your replies to Moodle from the different tasks. The deadline is tentatively Week 10; all tasks will be completed by Week 9, then you can curate your reflective learning journal with AI assistance.
